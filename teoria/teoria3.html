Teoría y Práctica: Tiempos de Presente para el Futuro

1. Usos de los Tiempos Presentes para el Futuro

En inglés, es común usar tiempos verbales del presente para hablar de acciones o eventos futuros. Cada tiempo verbal tiene un matiz de significado diferente.

Uso Estructura Ejemplo
Present Simple
Para eventos futuros programados (horarios, calendarios).		 Verbo en presente We have a meeting next Monday at 5 p.m.
Present Continuous
Para planes o citas futuras ya confirmadas.		 am/is/are + verbo -ing We are travelling to Paris next month.
Verbs + to + Infinitive
Para planes, esperanzas o intenciones futuras.		 plan/hope/intend + to + verbo I hope to arrange another visit with our suppliers.
Be + Adjective
Para describir disponibilidad futura.		 am/is/are + adjetivo I’m afraid they are busy until next Monday.

Nota sobre State Verbs: Verbos que describen estados (e.g., understand, know, like, mean, need) normalmente no se usan en Present Continuous, incluso si se refieren al futuro.

2. Rules for Using Articles

a/an (indefinite article)

Use a before words starting with a consonant sound: a badge, a desk.

Use an before words starting with a vowel sound: an airport, an economist.

We use a/an:

  • To talk about singular countable nouns in a general way (the person or thing could be one or many): an airport bus, a badge.
  • To talk about people’s jobs: he’s a manager, she’s an architect.
  • With singular, countable nouns: a company, a cup of coffee.

the (definite article)

We use the:

  • To talk about specific nouns (it’s clear to the listener which person or thing): the registration desk.
  • To talk about a noun for a second time: it’s the job I told you about last week.
  • With superlatives: It’s the largest hotel chain in the world.
  • With singular, plural, and uncountable nouns: the company, the companies, the information.
  • To say there is only one: She’s the Human Resources Manager of our company.
  • With natural places, regions: the Amazon River, the Pacific Ocean.
  • With groups of countries, states, and islands: the United Kingdom, the United States, the Philippines.
  • Periods of time: the weekend, the eleventh century.

no article

We use no article:

  • To talk about plural nouns and uncountable nouns in a general way: they hate delays, I like coffee.
  • With many proper nouns (e.g., single countries, continents, cities, languages, etc.): France, New York.
  • With work or home: He’s always at work by ten. He’s at home today.
  • With transportation: by taxi, by plane, by car, on foot.
  • With days of the week: Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday…

Some countries need «the» before their name because they are groups of:
